# Noetfield Systems Operating Plan

Date: 2026-06-05  
Primary market: Vancouver, BC and remote English-speaking SMBs  
Initial verticals: Construction and trades; property management  
Strategic wedge: revenue-generating AI operations implementation with TrustField governance embedded from day one

## Executive Mandate

Noetfield Systems will sell practical AI automation services to underserved SMB industries, beginning with construction/trades and property management. The company will use consulting and implementation revenue to discover repeatable operational workflows, convert those patterns into SaaS modules, and make TrustField the governance, audit, and compliance layer across every deployment.

The existing Noetfield public positioning is governance-grade AI for boards, with a ladder of Copilot QuickScan, Copilot Readiness, and Trust Ledger. This plan preserves that evidence-first posture while creating a more execution-led SMB revenue engine: audit, implement, manage, productize.

## Strategic Thesis

SMBs do not need generic AI strategy. They need backlog reduction, faster estimating, fewer missed follow-ups, cleaner handoffs, better documentation, fewer billing delays, and safer use of AI inside existing tools. Construction/trades and property management are attractive because they are operationally fragmented, document-heavy, communications-heavy, margin-sensitive, and underserved by enterprise AI vendors.

Noetfield should not begin as a pure SaaS startup. It should begin as an implementation company with strict pattern capture. Every project becomes a laboratory for workflow data, automation ROI, governance requirements, and eventual SaaS packaging.

## Market Facts To Anchor The Plan

- Canadian business AI adoption is still early enough for implementation-led firms to win. Statistics Canada reported 12.2% of businesses used AI to produce goods or deliver services in Q2 2025, up from 6.1% in Q2 2024. The Q2 2026 Canadian Survey on Business Conditions reported 19.2% of businesses using AI.
- The gap is not awareness; it is operationalization. SMBs need training, workflow redesign, risk controls, and integration into day-to-day systems.
- Construction remains a large Canadian GDP contributor. Statistics Canada's GDP by industry table should be used as the recurring source of truth for annual sizing.
- Vancouver is a concentrated local wedge. The City of Vancouver issued 5,146 total building permits in 2025, with total stated value of approximately CAD 6.16B, versus 4,919 permits and CAD 4.67B in 2024.
- Vancouver property management is under pressure from a softer but still expensive rental market. CMHC's 2025 Rental Market Report shows Vancouver purpose-built rental vacancy at 3.7%, the highest level since 1988, with average two-bedroom purpose-built rent at CAD 2,363 and condo two-bedroom rent at CAD 2,900.

## Opportunity Scoring Framework

Score each workflow 1-5 for the following:

| Dimension | Weight | Definition |
|---|---:|---|
| Time saved | 20% | Hours saved per month |
| Revenue impact | 20% | New revenue, retained revenue, faster cash, margin protection |
| Automation frequency | 15% | How often workflow runs |
| Implementation complexity | 15% | Lower complexity scores higher |
| Vertical applicability | 15% | Repeatable across many similar firms |
| Trust/risk differentiation | 10% | Needs evidence, approval, logs, compliance |
| Buyer urgency | 5% | Sponsor has immediate pain and budget |

Formula:

`Opportunity Score = weighted score total x confidence multiplier`

Confidence multiplier:

- 1.0 = measured baseline.
- 0.8 = credible estimate.
- 0.6 = anecdotal.
- 0.4 = unverified.

### Productization Thresholds

- Score 4.2+ across 5+ clients: SaaS MVP candidate.
- Score 3.8+ across 3+ clients: template and managed service candidate.
- Score below 3.0: custom service only unless strategic.

# Phase 5 - Technical Stack

## Multi-Tenant Architecture

### Principles

- Tenant data must be isolated by design.
- Every AI action must be traceable.
- Human approval must be explicit for high-impact actions.
- Integration permissions must be least-privilege.
- Client-specific prompts, files, embeddings, and logs must never bleed across tenants.

### Tenancy Model

Start with single database, shared schema, tenant_id on every row, row-level security enabled. Move high-sensitivity or enterprise tenants to separate database/schema later.

Core tables:

- tenants
- users
- roles
- integrations
- workflows
- automations
- automation_runs
- approvals
- documents
- embeddings
- audit_events
- decisions
- incidents
- kpi_measurements

## Workflow Engine

### Requirements

# Phase 8 - TrustField

## Product Definition

TrustField is the governance and compliance layer for SMB AI operations. It records what AI systems exist, what they access, what they produce, who approved key actions, what evidence supported the action, and what happened afterward.

## Audit Layer

### Audit Objects

| Object | Purpose |
|---|---|
| System register | List AI tools and automations |
| Data register | Data sources and sensitivity |
| Workflow register | What process automation supports |
| Execution log | What happened on each run |
| Decision log | Human/AI-supported decisions |
| Approval log | Who approved what and when |
| Exception log | Failures, overrides, incidents |
| Evidence pack | Source artifacts supporting claims |

## Governance Layer

### Control Baseline

- Owner assigned to every automation.
- Data sources documented.
- AI model/provider documented.
- Prompt/workflow version tracked.
- Human approval defined.
- External-send rules defined.
- Sensitive-data handling documented.
- Logs retained.
- Monthly review completed.
- Incident path defined.

## Decision Logs

Every high-impact workflow must log:

- Tenant.
- Workflow.
- Run ID.
- Trigger.
- Input references.
- AI model.
- Prompt/workflow version.
- Output.
- Confidence/validation result.
- Human reviewer.
- Approval/rejection.
- Final action.
- Timestamp.
- Evidence links.

High-impact examples:

- Sending a quote.
- Sending an invoice.
- Approving/rejecting tenant-related action.
- Assigning urgent maintenance.
- Sending owner/client report.
- Creating change order.

## Execution Traceability

Each automation run must answer:

1. What triggered the run?
2. What data was accessed?
3. What did the AI produce?
4. What rules were applied?
5. Was a human approval required?
6. Who approved or changed the output?
7. What external action occurred?
8. What evidence supports the action?
9. Were there exceptions?
10. What KPI impact was recorded?

## Compliance Framework

TrustField should be practical, not overbuilt. The first version should support:

- Privacy-by-design records.
- Data minimization.
- Role-based access.
- Human-in-the-loop controls.
- AI system inventory.
- Third-party vendor register.
- Incident/exception management.
- Exportable monthly governance report.
- Client-specific retention settings.

For property management, add:

- Tenant personal information controls.
- Fairness-sensitive decision restrictions.
- No autonomous rejection/approval of tenant applications without human review.
- Clear separation between draft recommendations and final decisions.

For construction/trades, add:

- Contract and change-order evidence.
- Safety-critical disclaimer and escalation.
- No autonomous legal or engineering determinations.
- Approval record for client-facing commercial commitments.
